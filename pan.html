Pathfinder for Autonomous Navigation

A team of 12-15 Cornell students built two cubesats as part of the Space Systems Design Studio research

Propulsion System Testing

I initially worked in a team of three on the propulsion system. We tested a 1U SLA 3D printed propulsion system in a thermal vacuum chamber. A 6 axis force/torque sensor provided thrust data and three thermocouples provided temperature data. After iterating on the nozzle design and o-ring placement, we ordered the final revisions to integrate with the rest of the flight hardware.

Modal Analysis









Before ordering a new revision for the structure, I analyzed all six of our solar panels to ensure they met our design requirements for launch. I modeled the panels in ANSYS and conducted a modal analysis to find the modes, deformation and equivalent stress due to the launch vehicle loads. Initially, the max deformation was 0.25mm which was larger than our max acceptable deformation of 0.1mm. This needed to be addressed so that panel components do not disconnect during launch. I then modified the locations of the mounting holes to reduce the max deformation to a more acceptable level (less than 0.1 mm).

Thermal Analysis

I modeled the thermal behavior of the PAN satellites in order to predict the worst case temperatures for each subsystem in orbit. In order to achieve an accurate simulation, I took the following steps:
1) Create a thermal vacuum chamber (TVC) thermal model in Thermal Desktop.
2) Validate the TVC thermal model by conducting tests with an aluminum block.
3) Create a thermal model of the PAN satellites in the TVC in Thermal Desktop.
4) Validate and tune the PAN satellite model parameters. Conduct hot and cold side tests with the ETU satellite in the TVC.
5) Create orbit performance predictions using the validated PAN satellite model.
